== {subtitle}

consider a more <<general>> grammar allowing all words

```yacc
<char> ::= "a" | "b" | "c" | "d" | "e" | "f" | "g" | "h" | "i" | "j" | "k" | "l" | "m" | "n" | "o" | "p" | "q" | "r" | "s" | "t" | "u" | "v" | "w" | "x" | "y" | "z"
<word> ::= <chars> " "
<chars> ::= <char> <chars> | <char>
<words> ::= <word> <words> | <word>
```

or, a more <<specific>> grammar, defining the exactly the words we are going to use

```yacc
<word> ::= "hello" | "world" | "bye"
<sentence> ::= <word> "." | <word> " " <sentence>
```

We know that the more specific grammar is NOT a subset of the more general grammar, due to the punctuation mark.
However, it would be cool to be able to reason about grammars in such a way.
In other words, if we removed the period (`<sentence> ::= <word> | <word> " " <sentence>`), could we prove that the specifc grammar is a subset of the more general grammar?
